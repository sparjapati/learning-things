## Visa / work authorization for Germany

- **EU Blue Card** is the standard route for skilled non-EU professionals. As of 2026: standard threshold is **€50,700 gross/year**; a reduced threshold of **€45,934.20 gross/year** applies to shortage occupations, which explicitly includes IT roles (ISCO-08 group 25 — software engineers, full-stack developers, Java developers, DevOps/cloud engineers). Only base salary counts toward this threshold — bonuses and variable pay don't.
- **IT specialists can qualify without a university degree** if they have at least 3 years of relevant professional experience within the last 7 years — relevant if your degree isn't CS-specific, or you don't have one at all.
- **Job Seeker Visa**: lets you enter Germany for up to 6 months specifically to search for work in person, converting to a work visa/Blue Card once you have an offer — useful if you'd rather be physically present for interviews/networking than apply purely from India.
- Confirm sponsorship willingness **early** in the process, not after an offer — see the scripted answer below.

## Comp & negotiation differences

- German salaries are quoted as **gross annual**, but the deductions work differently from an Indian CTC breakdown — German income tax, social security (health/pension/unemployment insurance), and church tax (if applicable) typically leave **55–65% of gross as take-home**, depending on tax bracket.
- A German gross number converted to INR at face exchange rate often looks unimpressive next to an Indian CTC figure — but German social security bundles healthcare and pension into the deduction in a way an Indian CTC breakdown doesn't; a raw currency conversion without adjusting for that is misleading.
- **"Remote job at a German company" and "job in Germany" are different tracks, with different pay bands.** A remote-from-India role at a company that can't easily open a local Indian entity is often paid on a distinct "global remote" band — sometimes above local India-market rates, but usually below what the same role pays a Germany-based employee. Clarify which track a listing actually is before assuming either the visa questions or the German pay band apply.

## Interview process differences

- The loop structure (recruiter screen → technical/coding round → system design or pairing → hiring manager/culture round) is broadly similar to what you already know, but German hiring processes are frequently reported as **slower-paced**, with more weeks between rounds than typical Indian or US timelines.
- **Notice periods** in German contracts commonly run 1–3 months (longer for senior/long-tenured roles) — standard and expected there, and not wildly different from a typical Indian notice period, but confirm your own contract's exact figure before quoting it in a negotiation.

## Language: do you need German to work in English?

- Plenty of English-first engineering roles exist, concentrated heavily in **Berlin** and at startups/scale-ups (Zalando, Personio, N26, HelloFresh, Delivery Hero, and many VC-backed startups) where the engineering team's working language is English by default, even though the company itself is German.
- More traditional/enterprise employers (Mittelstand firms, banks, industrial companies, government-adjacent roles) are far more likely to require German, both day-to-day and often in the interview process itself.
- Filtering through English-speaking-specific boards (EnglishJobs.de) or startup-heavy lists (Berlin Startup Jobs) is a more efficient filter than assuming every German-company listing is off-limits without the language.
- Even at English-first companies, basic German (A1/A2) is a real quality-of-life advantage outside work (visa office visits, apartment hunting, daily life) — not a hiring requirement, but worth factoring into your own decision, separately from what's needed to get hired.

## How to answer the visa/relocation/language questions

### "Will you need visa sponsorship?"

- **Weak** ❌: "I'm not sure, I haven't looked into it." — signals you haven't done basic homework on your own move, and leaves the interviewer to research it for you.
- **High-value** ✅: "Yes, I'd need sponsorship — I understand this would likely go through the EU Blue Card process, and given my role and experience I should qualify under the IT shortage-occupation threshold. I wanted to confirm early whether your company has sponsored Blue Cards before." ✅
- **Principle**: never let this be the first time you've thought about your own visa path. Naming the actual mechanism (Blue Card, shortage-occupation threshold) signals seriousness and surfaces a potential blocker early instead of after several rounds.

### "What's your German language level?"

- **Weak** ❌: "I don't speak any German, but I'm sure that won't be a problem." — dismissive, and presumes their team's actual working language without having asked.
- **High-value** ✅: "I don't currently speak German beyond basic phrases, though I'm committed to learning over time. I saw this role listed with English as the working language — can you confirm whether any day-to-day work would require German beyond that?" ✅
- **Principle**: don't guess at whether English is enough — ask directly. "English-first company" and "the specific team you'd join" aren't always the same thing; some teams or client-facing work inside an English-first company may still need German.

### "Are you open to relocating, or looking for fully remote?"

- **Weak** ❌: "I'm open to anything." — gives them nothing to plan around, and reads as not having thought through your own preference.
- **High-value** ✅: "My preference is [state it — e.g. 'remote initially, with openness to relocating once I'm settled into the role']. I'm flexible on the exact timeline if that helps your planning." ✅
- **Principle**: the same "state a preference, then offer flexibility" pattern as the interview-scheduling entry in [recruiter-and-interviewer-responses.md](recruiter-and-interviewer-responses.md) — pure openness under-signals your own priorities.

## Common gotchas

- **Converting German gross salary to INR at face exchange rate and comparing directly to your India CTC is misleading** — account for the ~35–45% typically lost to German tax/social security, and that healthcare/pension are bundled into that deduction rather than itemized separately the way an Indian CTC is.
- **Employer-of-Record (EOR) arrangements are common for remote-from-India hires** — a company without a local Indian entity may hire you through an EOR (e.g. Deel, Remote.com) instead of running any visa process at all. Don't confuse an EOR-based remote offer with the Blue Card/relocation path above; they're unrelated.
- **Don't assume every German-company listing requires German** — check the specific team/role, not just the country. The wrong assumption either way costs you: applying to roles you'd be filtered out of, or skipping roles you'd actually qualify for.
