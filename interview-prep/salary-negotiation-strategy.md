# How Much CTC Should You Ask For? (India Job-Switch Negotiation)

See also: [recruiter-and-interviewer-responses.md](recruiter-and-interviewer-responses.md) for how to *phrase* an answer when directly asked about your current/expected CTC or other offers — this file is about how to actually arrive at the number first.

## The core mistake: anchoring off in-hand instead of CTC

Negotiate off your current **CTC**, not your in-hand (take-home) pay. In-hand is usually only **~75–85% of CTC** — the gap is employer PF contribution, gratuity, insurance premiums, and variable/bonus components that count toward CTC but never hit your account as salary. Anchoring a new-offer ask against your in-hand figure understates your real current pay and makes your ask look smaller than it should. First step, always: figure out your actual current CTC from your last payslip/offer letter breakdown, not your bank balance.

## Hike-percentage benchmarks by switch type

Rather than trusting generic "average salary for X role" numbers (see gotcha below), a more reliable heuristic is the expected **hike percentage** for the specific kind of move you're making:

| Switch type | Typical hike (on CTC) |
|---|---|
| IT Services → IT Services | 20–35% |
| IT Services → Product company | 40–100% (structural pay gap between services and product firms) |
| Product → Product, same level | 25–40% |
| Product → Product, with a level bump | 35–60% |
| Specialized/high-demand stack (e.g. strong full-stack combos, AI/ML, cloud) | 50–100%, due to skill scarcity |

## Worked example

Say your current CTC is ₹25 LPA (after converting from in-hand as above):

- 30% hike → ~₹32.5 LPA
- 40% hike → ~₹35 LPA
- 50% hike → ~₹37.5 LPA

Pick the hike band that matches your actual switch type from the table, apply it to your *real* current CTC, then add roughly 10–15% headroom above your true target/floor — an initial ask typically gets negotiated down somewhat, so don't quote your minimum acceptable number as your opening ask.

## Where to actually check compensation data (by experience + skills, not just role title)

Most generic salary sites only filter by job title. These let you narrow by years of experience and, in some cases, your specific skill stack:

| Source | Best for | Filters by experience? | Filters by specific skills/stack? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Naukri Salary Predictor | Quick India-specific estimate | Yes — direct YOE input | Yes — direct skills input, alongside role/city |
| PayScale | Global data with skill-specific modifiers | Yes | Yes — has dedicated "[role] with skill: [X]" report pages |
| AmbitionBox | India-specific, per-company reported salaries + reviews | Yes — experience-range filter per company | Partially — searchable by designation, weaker on individual skills |
| Levels.fyi | Structured, verified data at product companies/MNCs/GCCs, with base/bonus/stock split | Indirectly — via "level," which maps roughly to YOE | Weak — mostly by role title, not tech stack |
| Glassdoor | Company-specific ranges + interview insights | Loosely | No, mostly by title |
| Blind / Grapevine | Real, current, anecdotal offers and negotiation threads at named companies | Informally, via posts | Informally, via posts |

Practical approach: start with Naukri Salary Predictor or PayScale for an estimate keyed to your actual stack and experience, cross-check AmbitionBox/Levels.fyi for your specific *target* companies, then skim Blind/Grapevine for very recent real numbers — aggregator tools lag actual market movement, especially for a fast-moving skill combo.

## Common gotchas

- **Generic "average salary for [role]" blog aggregators are unreliable** — they tend to be SEO content mills blending fresher-heavy and services-company-heavy samples, which skews the number well below what a strong candidate with in-demand skills at a product company actually commands. Treat them as a weak prior at best, not a benchmark; use the sources above instead.
- **City and company tier both move the number a lot** — Bangalore/Hyderabad/Pune carry a location premium; product companies and GCCs typically outpay services/consulting firms by 1.5–2x for the same experience level.
- **Don't quote your true floor as your opening ask** — leave negotiating room, since the number usually moves down from the initial ask, not up.
